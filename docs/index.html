Higher Education Levels Are Linked to Stronger Professional Skill Sets

Employee skill distributions vary noticeably across education levels in this workforce dataset.

By Myat Thinzar Maw · Dataset: Global Talent Market Dataset

The Data

This dataset contains 23,876 employee records describing workforce characteristics across multiple countries and industries. Each row represents an individual employee and includes information such as salary, years of experience, education level, industry, certifications, skills, and company size. The dataset was cleaned and enhanced through several stages. Salary values were standardized into numeric format, a career_stage variable was created from years of experience, and experience groups were generated using quantile-based categories. These additions support comparisons between employee characteristics and professional outcomes. The analysis focuses on how education levels relate to skills, certifications, experience, and compensation patterns across the workforce.

The Central Finding

To better understand compensation relative to experience, a derived variable called salary per year of experience was created by dividing annual salary by years of experience. This measure helps compare earnings across employees with different career lengths and provides a standardized way to evaluate compensation.

14,763.3 Median salary earned per year of experience (USD)

This statistic indicates that the typical employee in the dataset earns approximately $14,763 annually for each year of accumulated experience. The variable is measured in U.S. dollars per year of experience and helps account for differences in career length when comparing employees. Higher values indicate employees who earn relatively more for their level of experience, while lower values indicate employees earning less relative to their experience. The substantial variation observed throughout the dataset suggests that factors beyond experience, such as education, skills, certifications, and industry, may also influence employee compensation.

A Closer Look

Experience is generally associated with higher earnings, but the relationship is not always straightforward. The chart below examines salary and years of experience across thousands of employees. Look for the overall upward trend while also paying attention to the variation in salaries among employees with similar levels of experience.

Chart 1: Relationship between years of experience and annual salary (USD).

The scatter plot shows a clear positive relationship between experience and salary, with salary generally increasing as years of experience increase. However, substantial variation exists within each experience level, suggesting that factors beyond experience also contribute to employee earnings.

Across Groups

Education is often associated with professional development and skill acquisition. To explore this relationship, the ridge plot compares the distribution of reported skills across education levels. If education contributes to stronger skill development, higher education groups should display distributions shifted toward larger skill counts.

Chart 2: Distribution of reported skills across education levels.

The ridge plot reveals noticeable differences among education groups. Employees with Master’s and PhD degrees tend to report higher skill counts, while lower education groups are more concentrated around smaller values. These differences provide stronger evidence that educational attainment is associated with broader professional skill development.

What This Means

The findings suggest that education is associated with meaningful differences in workforce skills. While salary generally increases with experience, experience alone does not explain all variation in employee outcomes. Employees with similar experience levels often earn substantially different salaries.

The ridge plot provides stronger evidence for the report’s central claim. Employees with advanced degrees consistently demonstrate higher concentrations of reported skills than employees with lower educational attainment. These patterns suggest that education may contribute to the development of broader professional capabilities that support career growth.

Although the dataset reveals clear associations, it does not establish causation. Future research could examine how industry, occupation, certifications, and geographic location influence the relationship between education, skills, and compensation.
